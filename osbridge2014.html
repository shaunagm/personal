Obstacle #1: Picking a Project

  • Depending on definition, either thousands or millions of OSS projects
  • Maintainers may not consider this element of experience because contributors have already chosen their project
  • Big barrier: many attendees would rather we point them to a project
  • We tend to present students a pre-selected group of projects (esp those worked on by volunteers)
  • We also teach how to pick a project

  1. activity

Read more about our "Finding a Project" activity at
bit.ly/finding-a-project.

  • (Go over elements of evaluation)
  • This is advice to newcomers, but useful for maintainers as well.
  • How would you evaluate your own project? Is it big or small? How responsive? What interests and values do your community members share?
  • Make this information explicit. Put it on your website, documentation, IRC channel topic, code of conduct.
  • Help good matches select in and bad matches select out.
  • This is something that the maintainers of Dreamwidth emphasized in their talk yesterday - I very much agree!
  • Find out if it's working by asking newcomers why they've chosen your project.

Obstacle #2: Understanding issues.

  • Learning how to read an issue tracker is HARD.
  • Most issues are written for maintainers, not newcomers.
  • They take stuff for granted.
  • Need to spell this stuff out before handing it to newcomers.
  • We didn't use to do this!
  • Our aggregator lets you look at trackers for lots of projects, and sory by easy.
  • We used to present these, with a minimum of filtering, to students.
  • Terrible idea. Nothing more demoralizing than not being able to fix something easy.

Obstacle #3: Setting up the development environment.

"lots of downloads, need an approved account to have access… had to create own database on localhost… tears clouding vision"

  • Not all contributions involve setting up environments, but for those that do: a big deal!
  • Story of Purdue.
  • Happy ending: Privly, led by Jen Davidson, did documentation sprint where they fixed over two dozen issues.
  • But most newcomers who fail at this fail silently, and projects can't learn what specifically is causing it.

From the In-Person Events Handbook:

  1. What virtual environments or development kits you need
  2. What dependencies you need
  3. How to download the source
  4. Which version to download
  5. How to build/compile the project
  6. How to test your changes
  7. How to contribute your changes back to the project

Also:

  1. Contact information for community/maintainers
  2. Overview of the project
  3. As much documentation as possible
  • Again, these suggestions are both info for maintainers to provide, and questions a newcomer can use to help find their way when information is not provided.

Need help? Try a setup sprint.

  • Sound like a lot? We can help.
  • We do IRC based setup sprints with a mix of testers.
  • Great way to get real time feedback you can fix quickly.

Obstacle #4: Anxiety and Self-Doubt

  • Not just technical obstacles: psychological ones to.
  • Insecurities can make people hesitate to dip their toes into open source.

  • I'm a burden on project maintainers if I ask for help.
  • No one has responded to my question because it is stupid.
  • I'm having trouble contributing because I'm incompetent.
  • I'm not a coding genius so I have nothing to contribute.
  • I'm not a coder so I have nothing to contribute.

  • I've heard these kinds of comments countless times at events.
  • These are reasonable things to think! These are stereotypes that exist out in the world.
  • (And newcomers do have limitations.)
  • Key is to validate feelings while also helping newcomers feel like they belong.
  • For instance, when students say, "I'm having trouble contributing because I'm incompetent." I say, "I totally understand that feeling! I think that all the time. But then I see people who've been contributing for a decade longer than me have trouble with a tool like git or installing a project and I think, no, everyone's always learning. The constant challenges and growth are part of what I love about open source."

Notice a pattern?

  • I already spoiled this, but there's a pattern to my advice.
  • Make implicit knowledge explicit!
  • And use newcomers to do this.
  • It turns out newcomers have this kind of superpower.
  • They can tell when projects are inaccessible to newcomers!
  • (Sounds obvious, huh?)
  • Engage newcomers and let them know how valuable they are.
  • But maybe you don't have newcomers. Maybe what you have is yourself.
  • Great news! You were a newcomer once too.

The Activity (finally!)

  • Channel our inner newcomers!
  • By doing so, we'll uncover implicit knowledge
  • And brainstorm ways and share resources to make it explicit.

You need:

  1. a piece of paper
  2. a pencil or pen
  3. a partner
  • Turn to the person next to you. They're your partner. At least one of you should have made at least one contribution to an open source project.
  • Doesn't matter the kind or if you've literally made only one contribution.
  • If you and your partners are both newcomers, raise your hands!
  • (Pause for pairing)
  • Okay, here's the overview. Activity consists of 3 steps. Each will take about five minutes. You'll write down answers to a series of questions.
  • Then your parter will ask you the questions I give you out loud. You'll explain, and your partner will tell you whether or not they understand. If they don't understand, try again.

Step 1

Think of a contribution you made.

  • How did you find out about the project?
  • How did you figure out if your contribution was good/correct/appropriate?

  • How did you pick a part of it to work on?
  • How did you set up the project?
  • How did you find the parts of the project you needed to add to or alter?
The first set of questions, in bold, are the ones you should definitely get to. The other questions, not in bold, are if you finish with the first questions before the five minutes are up. If you don't understand a question, please feel free to ask me for clarification.

Step 2

What did you need to know?

  • What technical knowledge did you need?
  • What kind of social knowledge did you need?

  • What open source tools did you need to use? Did you need to use version control, issue trackers, IRC?
  • What domain knowledge did you need?
  • What technical knowledge did you need? Did you need to know a language/framework/module?
  • What process knowledge did you need? How did you figure out what to do at each step?
  • What kind of social knowledge did you need? Did you need to learn how to follow up with someone politely but persistently? Did you need to figure out who the maintainer was?
  • What domain knowledge did you need? For a music project, what did you need to know about music? For a scientific open source project, what did you need to know about the experimental method or chemistry or statistics?

Step 3

How did you learn it?

  • What resources did you use? Project documentation? Tutorials? Stack overflow?
  • Who did you ask for help? Friends? An IRC channel or mailing list? A professor?

  • What resources did you wish you had?
  • What was the hardest thing to learn? What are you most grateful you already knew before starting?

Sharing time!

I'd love to hear some volunteers take a moment to talk about the contribution they made, the explicit and implicit knowledge they needed, and the tools they used - or wish they could have used - to get that knowledge.
If you enjoyed this activity and found any part of it valuable, I encourage you to take the contribution you just wrote about and submit it to MergeStories. This is something that newcomers at events can use to get a mental picture of what contributing is actually like, and to see that everyone - experienced people and newcomers alike - struggle with contributing sometimes.
